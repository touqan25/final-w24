Palestinian Desserts

Make Font Accessible

Ghraybeh

غريبة

Ghraybeh (the Arabic word for swooning) are Palestinian shortbread cookies consisting of three simple ingredients: Ghee, flour, and powdered sugar -- optionally flavored with orange blossom water, pistachios, and/or almonds. These cookies are traditionally made during religious holidays such as Ramadan, Eid, Christmas, or Easter. These cookies are simply one of the easiest dishes to make, and was one of the first dessert recipes I learned as a child.

Harisa

حريصة

Also referred to as basbousa or namoura depending on the region. Not to mistaken for the North African spicy red sauce, Palestinian Harisa is a sweet rose-water syrup soaked semonlina cake often topped with almond slivers. It is typically served in small diamond or square-shaped servings. Regional variations include the addition of shaved coconut or orange blossom. This dish comes together in one bowl/pan and is perfect for parties. The semonlina offers up a unique soft and crumbly texture that soaks up the syrup flavors perfectly.

Knafeh Nabulsiyeh

كنافة نابلسية

The renowned dessert of my hometown Nablus, Knafeh Nabulsiyeh. Notable for its distinct bright orange color, this dish consists of Nabulsi cheese (mild, white, stretchy cheese) and shredded wheat drenched in a orange blossom sugar syrup and topped with crushed pistachios. One of the fondest memories of my childhood is sharing a tray of Knafeh with my family in Amman, the children fighting for the biggest, most syrupy piece! Knafeh is the center of many heated debates: Is it pronounced knafeh, or chnafeh (definitely the former, but don't tell any country folk I said that!)? The pride of my hometown, Knafeh, to me, symbolizes just how much food fuels the soul -- connecting us to others across all difference.

Ma'amoul

معمول

Ma'amoul is a cookie made from semolina flour, stuffed with a variety of fillings such as date paste, pastachios, or walnuts. These cookies are most commonly made in preparation for Eid, to be served with Arabic coffee to guests. They are known for their distinctive shapes and intricate designs, traditionally achieved using special wooden molds called tabe. It is typical to hear a hearty whack come from the kitchen as the cookies are released from their molds. These cookies require a lot of patience and love, because they can take anywhere from 8 hours to 3 days to make! I can assure you it is well worth the wait.

Qatayef

قطايف

Qatayef is a staple dessert for Palestinians during the holy month of Ramadan. These fried pancakes are most commonly stuffed with cheese, walnuts, or ashta (a sweet cream). I consider them like the Middle East's version of a sweet dumpling. They have a deliciously crunchy exterior and often a soft luxurious interior, making it the perfect decadant treat to celebrate a successful fast. It is my dad's favorite dessert, my mom has to beg him to stop making them because he'll never stop!

Sahlab

سحلب

Sahlab is Middle Eastern milk pudding, made by simmering a mixture of milk, sugar, and cornstarch on the stove until thick, For flavoring my dad and I prefer a mixture of rose and orange blossom water, topping our servings with cinnamon and shaved coconut. It is the perfect pick-me-up on a cold winter night. One of the most salient memories of my childhood is walking the cold streets of Amman in December 2009 when my father, cousin, and I stopped by a sahleb vendor. The old man was kind and offered me an extra large portion, giving me a toothy smile as I took a big gulp. This drink reminds me of my people's kindness and hospitality.
